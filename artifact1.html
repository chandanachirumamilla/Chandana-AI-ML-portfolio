Artifact 01 · Machine Learning Fundamentals

Evolution of AI
— A Historical Timeline

Eighty years of artificial intelligence, from the first mathematical model of a neuron to today's agentic, multimodal systems — organized into five eras and framed for the audience most likely to find it useful: people hiring for this field.

Why I'm sharing this

As I start building a career in AI and machine learning, I wanted my portfolio to open with something that shows I understand the arc of this field — not just today's tools, but how the industry got here. This timeline traces AI's development from the first neural network models in 1943 through the generative AI era of 2026, including the field's two "AI winters" and the companies leading the space right now.

For recruiters and hiring managers, I think this matters for a simple reason: anyone can learn to prompt a chatbot, but understanding why the field looks the way it does — why certain approaches failed, why funding dried up twice, why deep learning didn't take off until hardware caught up with theory — signals a more durable kind of literacy. It's the difference between knowing the tools and understanding the terrain they came from.

The timeline

Five eras, 1943 – 2026

The color of the line itself tracks the field's temperature — cool and theoretical in the early decades, warming toward the mainstream, high-adoption era we're in now.

1943 – 1958

Early Foundations

From theory to the first learning machines.

1943
The First Neural Network Model Warren McCulloch and Walter Pitts publish a paper describing artificial neurons, laying the mathematical groundwork for neural networks.
1950
The Turing Test Alan Turing proposes a way to evaluate machine intelligence, asking whether machines can think.
1950
Theseus the Maze-Solving Mouse Claude Shannon builds an early machine-learning device — a robotic mouse that learns to navigate a maze.
1956
The Dartmouth Conference John McCarthy coins the term "artificial intelligence," widely considered the founding event of the field.
1958
The Perceptron Frank Rosenblatt develops the first artificial neural network capable of learning to classify simple patterns.
1966 – 1980

The First AI Winter

Early promises outrun what the technology could deliver.

1966
ELIZA Joseph Weizenbaum's ELIZA imitates a psychotherapist using simple pattern matching, convincing some users it truly understood them.
1969
Perceptrons (the book) Minsky and Papert mathematically expose the limits of single-layer neural networks, cooling research for over a decade.
1973
The Lighthill Report A UK government report concludes AI has not delivered on its goals, leading to major funding cuts.
1974–80
DARPA Pulls Back U.S. defense funding for AI research is drastically reduced, deepening the freeze known as the first AI Winter.
1980 – 1990s

Expert Systems and the Second AI Winter

A commercial resurgence, followed by another collapse.

1980
XCON Digital Equipment Corporation deploys a rule-based expert system to configure orders, saving an estimated $40 million a year.
1982
Japan's Fifth Generation Project Japan launches a national effort to build computers that reason like experts, sparking a global funding race.
1987
The LISP Machine Crash Specialized AI hardware collapses almost overnight as cheaper general-purpose workstations take over.
1990s
Expert Systems Fade Maintaining thousands of hand-written rules proves too costly, and funding is cut again, triggering a second AI Winter.
1997 – 2016

The Deep Learning Revolution

Hardware finally catches up with theory.

1997
Deep Blue Beats Kasparov IBM's Deep Blue defeats reigning world chess champion Garry Kasparov.
'86/'06
Backpropagation to "Deep Learning" Rumelhart, Hinton, and Williams popularize backpropagation in 1986; by 2006, Hinton's team revives it under the name "deep learning."
2006–10
Cloud Computing Arrives AWS, then Google Cloud and Azure, make massive on-demand computing available for rent.
2012
AlexNet Wins ImageNet A deep neural network trained on Nvidia GPUs nearly halves the image-recognition error rate.
2016
AlphaGo Beats Lee Sedol DeepMind's AlphaGo defeats a world Go champion years earlier than experts predicted possible.
2017 – 2026

The Generative AI Era

Language models move from research labs into daily life.

2017
"Attention Is All You Need" Google researchers introduce the Transformer architecture, the foundation for nearly every major language model since.
2020
GPT-3 OpenAI releases a 175-billion-parameter language model whose fluency surprises even researchers in the field.
Nov 2022
ChatGPT Launches ChatGPT reaches roughly 100 million users within two months, pulling generative AI into mainstream use.
2023–26
Multimodal, Agentic AI Google, OpenAI, Anthropic, Meta, and others release models that reason across text, images, and code, and act as agents.
Where things stand

Leading AI models and companies today

Knowing the competitive landscape is table stakes for anyone entering this field.

Company Flagship Model Known For

Behind the artifact

Process & reflection

My Process

I built this timeline for a Machine Learning Fundamentals course, researching milestones across multiple sources and organizing them into eras that tell a coherent story rather than just a list of dates. I used Claude (Anthropic) to help research milestone dates and organize the material — but the judgment calls about what to include, how to group eras, and how to frame the narrative were mine, and I reviewed everything for accuracy before finalizing it.

Lessons Learned

Curation is harder than research. Deciding what to leave out of an 80-year history was tougher than finding the facts in the first place.

Audience changes everything. The version I built for a class assignment isn't the version that belongs in a professional portfolio — repackaging it for recruiters meant explaining why it matters to them, not just what happened.

Value Proposition

This artifact shows that I can take a large, technical subject and organize it into something a busy reader can actually absorb — without losing the substance. That's a skill that transfers directly: distilling research, summarizing a model's behavior, or explaining a technical decision to a non-technical stakeholder all require the same instinct for what to keep and what to cut.

Unique Value

Rather than listing dates, I organized the timeline around why each era ended the way it did — funding collapses, hardware limits, a single paper that changed the field's direction. That narrative framing is what separates a fact sheet from something that actually helps someone understand the field.

Relevance

For recruiters and hiring managers in AI/ML, this artifact signals a candidate who understands the field's trajectory, not just its current tools — useful in roles that require explaining model decisions, evaluating new techniques against past failures, or communicating AI strategy to non-technical leadership.
