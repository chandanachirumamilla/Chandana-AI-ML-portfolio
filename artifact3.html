Artifact 03 · Group Project, AIML-500

Is This Problem
Actually AI-Suited?

A business case analysis asking whether Google Shopping's Merchant Center could become an AI-powered decision-support platform for retailers — and whether AI is genuinely the right tool for that job, or just the trendy one.

AI Feasibility Analysis Business Case Development Stakeholder Analysis Technical Writing Research & Citation

Why I'm sharing this

My first two artifacts showed research synthesis and hands-on building. This one shows something different again: the ability to evaluate whether a problem is actually a good fit for AI in the first place — a judgment call that matters more than people expect, since not every business problem needs a machine learning solution just because one is fashionable.

For AI product and strategy teams, I think this is a core skill that's easy to undervalue: before anyone builds anything, someone has to make the case — with evidence, not enthusiasm — that AI is the right tool for a specific problem, and be honest about where it might not be.

This was a group project completed with classmates for AIML-500. My contribution focused specifically on the AI Opportunity and Why AI Is a Good Fit sections of the analysis — evaluating the underlying problem against standard criteria for AI suitability. The write-up below reflects that contribution; the full team project also covered the business problem framing, financial value modeling, data requirements, and a phased rollout plan.
The context

The gap in Google Shopping

Merchant Center tells sellers what already happened — impressions, clicks, conversion rate. It doesn't tell them what to do next: what to price a product, how much inventory to buy, or which listings are worth advertising. Our team explored whether that gap could be closed with an AI-powered recommendation layer sitting on top of data Google already collects.

Source: Google, Shopping Graph scale update, Google I/O 2026

My contribution

Testing the problem against AI-fit criteria

Rather than assuming AI was the right approach, I evaluated the problem against five standard criteria for whether a business problem is genuinely suited to an AI solution.

Criterion 1

Historical data exists

Google already collects rich data through Merchant Center and Google Ads — impressions, click-through rates, conversions, pricing history, and reviews across millions of listings.

Criterion 2

Patterns exist in the data

Shopping behavior follows recognizable rhythms — seasonal spikes, regional preferences, and predictable product lifecycle curves — exactly what ML models are built to detect.

Criterion 3

Predictions create real value

Knowing in advance which products will trend or what price will convert directly changes purchasing and ad decisions before money is spent — not an academic exercise.

Criterion 4

Success can be measured

Impact shows up in metrics merchants already track — revenue, conversion rate, inventory turnover, and return on ad spend — so before-and-after comparison is straightforward.

Criterion 5

AI offers a genuine advantage

Across millions of SKUs and merchants, manual analysis at this scale simply isn't possible — this is the difference between a task that can be done at all and one that can't.

What AI would actually predict

From signals to specific predictions

Part of my analysis involved translating "AI could help here" into specific, answerable predictions — since a business case needs concrete outputs, not just a general endorsement of the technology.

1.9%

Average Google Shopping conversion rate — the gap an AI recommendation layer is positioned to close.

15–30%

Share of retail inventory industry-wide that sits dead or slow-moving — the target for demand forecasting.

$1.7T

Estimated global cost of inventory distortion annually — stockouts and overstock combined.

Demand

Expected future demand per product

Flagging products likely to underperform before a merchant commits capital to inventory — directly targeting the estimated 15–30% of retail inventory that sits dead or slow-moving industry-wide.

Pricing

The price point that balances margin and conversion

Recommending prices calibrated to lift conversion above the roughly 1.9% industry average for Google Shopping, without giving away margin unnecessarily.

Inventory

The stock level that avoids both extremes

Right-sizing inventory to reduce stockouts and overstock — addressing a documented, multi-billion-dollar global inventory-distortion problem.

Ad Spend

Where advertising budget should actually go

Shifting budget away from low-demand products toward listings most likely to convert, improving return on ad spend across a merchant's full catalog.

Challenges & Solutions

Challenge: Google doesn't publish an official merchant count, success-rate baseline, or internal performance data — so I couldn't back the AI-fit case with company-specific numbers, which made it tempting to either overstate confidence or fall back on vague claims.

Solution: I anchored the analysis in independently sourced, cited industry benchmarks instead — Google's own published Shopping Graph scale figures, third-party inventory-distortion research, and published conversion-rate averages — and was explicit in the writeup about which numbers were verified facts versus illustrative estimates. That distinction mattered more than making the case sound more certain than the evidence supported.

Value Proposition

This artifact shows I can evaluate whether AI is actually the right solution to a business problem — using a structured framework rather than intuition — and back that judgment with cited, verifiable evidence instead of assumptions.

Unique Value

Rather than defaulting to "AI can help with this," I treated AI-suitability as a hypothesis to test against explicit criteria, and was willing to flag where the evidence was strong (data availability, measurability) versus where it rested on illustrative assumptions (the exact scale of financial upside).

Relevance

For AI product and strategy teams, this shows the kind of judgment needed before a single model gets built: separating problems where AI creates real value from problems where it's just a trend being applied without justification.

Lessons Learned

A good AI case is honest about its own uncertainty. Some of the most useful sentences I wrote in this analysis were the ones admitting what we didn't know — like Google's actual merchant count — rather than smoothing over the gap.

Feasibility criteria prevent motivated reasoning. Working from a fixed checklist (data, patterns, value, measurability, advantage) made it much harder to talk myself into "AI would obviously help here" without actually checking each box.
